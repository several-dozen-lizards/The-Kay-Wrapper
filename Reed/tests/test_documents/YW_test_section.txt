The yurt was quiet except for the sound of Mattie breathing and the occasional pop from the fire in the central hearth. Moonlight came in pale stripes through the smoke hole overhead, laying silver lines across the felt walls and the thick carpets Delia had brought from her mother's house.

Delia sat cross-legged near the fire, close enough to feel the heat on her face but far enough that the smoke didn't make her eyes water. She had a bowl of warm water in her lap and a bundle of sage she'd been holding for the better part of an hour, turning it over in her hands like she was trying to read something written in the leaves.

Mattie was asleep on the other side of the hearth, bundled in blankets that made her look smaller than she was. She'd been running a fever for three days now, and Delia had been running herself ragged trying to keep it from getting worse. The fever had broken this morning, but Mattie still looked drawn and pale, her hair plastered to her forehead with sweat.

Delia dipped the sage in the water and wrung it out, then brought it to her nose and breathed in. The smell was sharp and clean, and it cut through the fog in her head like a knife. She'd been up for two days straight, dozing in fits and starts but never really sleeping, and she could feel the exhaustion settling into her bones like cold water.

She set the sage aside and reached for the small clay pot she kept her medicines in. Most of what she had were simple things—willow bark for pain, mint for upset stomachs, yarrow to stop bleeding. She'd learned it all from her mother, who'd learned it from her mother, and so on back through the generations until the knowledge disappeared into the murk of time.

Her mother had told her once that their family had always been healers, that it was a gift that ran in the blood. Delia wasn't sure she believed in gifts like that, but she knew she had a knack for it. She could look at someone and know what was wrong with them, could feel it in her gut like a pulled thread. It wasn't magic, not really—just observation and instinct and a little bit of luck.

She pulled out a small jar of honey and a twist of paper that held crushed garlic. Mattie hated the taste of it, but it would help keep the fever from coming back. Delia mixed it with a spoonful of honey and set it aside, then leaned back on her hands and stared up at the smoke hole.

The stars were out tonight, sharp and bright against the black sky. She could see the Shepherd's Crook and the Weaver's Loom, constellations her father had taught her when she was small. He'd been a horse trader, always on the move, and he'd known the stars like other men knew the roads. He'd died when she was twelve, thrown from a spooked mare, and her mother had never been quite the same after that.

Delia had been sixteen when she met Mattie. They'd both been working in the same market town—Delia selling herbs and medicines, Mattie selling jewelry she'd made from scavenged copper wire and broken glass. They'd circled each other for weeks, trading glances and small talk, until finally Mattie had come over to Delia's stall and asked if she had anything for a headache.

Delia had given her willow bark tea and a long look, and Mattie had smiled at her, sharp and knowing, and said, "You're staring."

"You're worth staring at," Delia had said, which was bold for her, and Mattie had laughed, surprised, and that had been the beginning of it.

They'd been together for five years now. They'd saved up enough to buy this yurt and a small herd of goats, and they'd made a life here on the edge of the grasslands, where the land was cheap and no one asked too many questions. It was a good life, quiet and steady, and Delia had been happy.

But lately she'd been feeling restless. She couldn't put her finger on why. Maybe it was the way the seasons had been changing—winters getting colder, summers hotter, the rains coming at strange times. Maybe it was the stories she'd been hearing from the traders who passed through, stories about unrest in the cities and strange sightings in the mountains. Or maybe it was just that she was getting older and starting to feel the weight of time, the sense that her life was moving forward whether she was ready or not.

She shook her head and stood up, moving over to where Mattie was sleeping. She knelt down and touched her forehead lightly, feeling for heat. Mattie's skin was cool and dry, and Delia let out a breath she hadn't realized she'd been holding.

"You're going to be fine," she murmured, more to herself than to Mattie. "You're going to be just fine."

Mattie stirred in her sleep, turning onto her side, and Delia reached out and tucked the blankets more tightly around her. Then she stood and moved back to the fire, settling down with her back against one of the support poles.

She was tired, bone-tired, but she didn't think she could sleep. Her mind was too full, thoughts chasing each other in circles. She picked up the sage again and held it under her nose, breathing in the sharp scent, trying to clear her head.

Outside, the wind picked up, rattling the felt walls and making the fire gutter. Delia pulled her shawl more tightly around her shoulders and stared into the flames, watching the way they twisted and danced, always moving, never still.

She thought about her mother, who had died three years ago, quickly and quietly in her sleep. Her mother had been a small, fierce woman with calloused hands and a sharp tongue, and she'd taught Delia everything she knew about healing. But she'd also taught her other things—how to read people, how to listen to the silence between words, how to trust her instincts even when they didn't make sense.

"Trust yourself," her mother had said, near the end, when she was fading and knew it. "You've got good instincts, girl. Don't second-guess them."

Delia had tried to live by that, but it was hard. She was always second-guessing herself, always wondering if she was doing the right thing. It was easier to trust her instincts when it came to healing—she could see the results, could measure success in fevers that broke and wounds that closed. But when it came to her own life, to the choices she made for herself and Mattie, it was harder. There were too many variables, too many things she couldn't control.

The fire popped again, sending up a shower of sparks. Delia watched them rise and disappear into the smoke, and she thought about the stars outside, cold and distant and unchanging. She wondered if the stars ever felt restless, if they ever looked down at the world and wanted to be somewhere else.

She smiled a little at the thought, shaking her head. She was getting maudlin, which was what happened when she didn't sleep. She needed to rest, needed to let her body recover, but she couldn't seem to make herself lie down. There was something in the air tonight, something that made her feel like she needed to stay awake, to keep watch.

Mattie mumbled something in her sleep, a string of words that Delia couldn't make out. She sounded restless, caught in a dream, and Delia moved over to her again, kneeling down and touching her shoulder gently.

"Shh," she murmured. "I'm here. You're safe."

Mattie quieted, her breathing evening out, and Delia stayed there for a moment, her hand resting on Mattie's shoulder. She felt the rise and fall of Mattie's breath, steady and strong, and something in her chest loosened.

"You're going to be fine," she said again, softer this time. "We're both going to be fine."

She didn't know if she believed it, but she said it anyway, because sometimes saying something out loud was enough to make it feel true.

She stood and moved back to the fire, settling down again with her back to the pole. The wind was still rattling the walls, and she could hear the goats shifting restlessly in their pen outside. She closed her eyes and let herself drift, not quite sleeping but not quite awake, hovering in that strange in-between space where thoughts and dreams tangled together.

She dreamed about her mother, standing in a field of tall grass with her hands on her hips, looking up at the sky. Her mother turned to look at her, and her face was calm and untroubled, and she said, "It's coming, girl. Be ready."

Delia woke with a start, her heart pounding. The fire had burned down to coals, and the yurt was darker now, the moonlight thinner. She sat up and looked around, disoriented, trying to shake off the dream.

It had felt so real. She could still see her mother's face, could still hear her voice, low and steady. Be ready. But ready for what?

She stood and moved to the entrance of the yurt, pulling aside the felt flap and looking out. The night was still and quiet, the sky full of stars. The goats were settled now, no longer restless, and there was no sound except for the wind moving through the grass.

But something felt different. She couldn't put her finger on it, but there was a tension in the air, a sense of something waiting just out of sight. She stood there for a long moment, scanning the horizon, but there was nothing to see.

Finally, she let the flap fall and moved back inside, settling down by the fire again. She picked up a piece of wood and fed it to the coals, watching as the flames caught and began to grow. The warmth felt good, and she held her hands out to it, trying to chase away the chill that had settled into her bones.

Mattie stirred again, and this time she opened her eyes, blinking groggily.

"Delia?" she said, her voice rough with sleep.

"I'm here," Delia said, moving over to her. "How are you feeling?"

"Tired," Mattie said. "But better." She tried to sit up, but Delia put a hand on her shoulder, stopping her.

"Stay down," she said. "You need to rest."

"I've been resting for three days," Mattie said, but she didn't try to sit up again. She looked at Delia, her eyes sharp even in the dim light. "You look like hell."

"Thanks," Delia said dryly.

"When's the last time you slept?"

"I've been sleeping."

"Liar."

Delia smiled a little, despite herself. "I'll sleep when you're better."

"I am better," Mattie said. She reached out and took Delia's hand, squeezing it gently. "I'm okay. You can stop worrying."

"I'm not worrying."

"You're always worrying," Mattie said, but her voice was gentle, not accusing. "It's one of your more charming qualities."

Delia squeezed her hand back, then stood and moved over to where she'd left the honey and garlic mixture. She brought it back to Mattie and held it out.

"Eat this," she said.

Mattie made a face. "Do I have to?"

"Yes."

"I hate you."

"No, you don't."

Mattie sighed and took the spoon, swallowing the mixture quickly and then grimacing. "That's disgusting."

"It'll keep the fever from coming back."

"I'd rather have the fever."

"No, you wouldn't."

Mattie handed the spoon back, then lay down again, pulling the blankets up to her chin. "You're a tyrant," she said, but there was affection in her voice.

"And you're a terrible patient," Delia said. She leaned down and pressed a kiss to Mattie's forehead, then stood and moved back to the fire.

Mattie watched her for a moment, then said, "Something's bothering you."

Delia didn't answer right away. She stared into the fire, watching the flames dance, and thought about the dream, about her mother's voice. Be ready.

"I don't know," she said finally. "Maybe. I just—I feel like something's coming. I don't know what, but something."

"Something bad?"

"I don't know," Delia said again. She looked over at Mattie, who was watching her with those sharp, knowing eyes. "Maybe I'm just tired."

"Maybe," Mattie said, but she didn't sound convinced. She was quiet for a moment, then said, "Whatever it is, we'll handle it. We always do."

Delia nodded, but she didn't say anything. She turned back to the fire and fed it another piece of wood, watching as the flames grew higher, casting flickering shadows on the walls.

Outside, the wind picked up again, and somewhere in the distance, a wolf howled, long and lonely. Delia listened to the sound, feeling something in her chest tighten, and she thought again about her mother's words.

Be ready.

She didn't know what was coming, but she had a feeling she was about to find out.
